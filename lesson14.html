Understanding Nutrition Terms

Unit Learning Objectives:

  • 2.7 Differentiate between energy and nutrient density.
  • 2.3 Differentiate between macro and micronutrients
  • 2.2 Classify nutrients as essential or nonessential

Before we move on to nutrition guidelines and the connection of diet to disease, it is helpful to understand some terms you will frequently see in this course.

Calorie:

the energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water through 1 degree Celsius. What you see on food labels is kilocalories (kcal), and a kilocalorie is 1,000 calories. However, these two terms are often used interchangeably. Carbohydrates, protein, fat, and alcohol provide calories to the diet.

Diet:

The kinds and amounts of food that a person typically eats. Everyone has a diet, which does not always refer to weight loss or a medical eating plan.

Energy:

the capacity to do work. With nutrition, through the process of digestion, we convert the food we eat to energy. This food energy is calculated as Calories (C) or kilocalories (kcal). Our body metabolizes carbohydrates, proteins, and fats to provide energy.

Energy Dense:

A high calorie content in relation to its weight.

Essential Nutrient:

compounds that the body cannot make or cannot make in sufficient quantity. Thus, we must consume them in foods.

Health Coach:

is a mentor and partner in an individual's behavior change process. They are generally not credentialed to provide evidence-based nutrition advice or translate nutrition science to practical use.

Organic Nutrient:

A nutrient that contains both carbon and hydrogen, and sometimes nitrogen. Organic nutrients can be made by living organisms and are complex. In a sense, they are "alive", and therefore can be destroyed or broken down. Organic nutrients include the macronutrients - carbohydrates, protein, and fat.

Inorganic Nutrient:

A nutrient that does not contain carbon and hydrogen and is not created or destroyed. Inorganic nutrients are already in their simplest form, so they are not digested. Inorganic nutrients include minerals and water.

Macronutrient:

A nutrient required in large amounts in the diet; macronutrients also yield energy (calories). Macronutrients include carbohydrates, protein, and fat.  * Water is also a macronutrient in the sense that the body needs it in large amounts, but unlike the other macronutrients, it does not contain carbon or yield energy.

Micronutrient:

a chemical element or substance required in small amounts for normal growth and development. Micronutrients include vitamins and minerals.

Non-essential Nutrient:

A nutrient in which the body is capable of producing in sufficient amounts. These are not required in the diet but are no less important for health than essential nutrients.

Nutrient:

a substance that provides nourishment essential for growth and the maintenance of life.

Nutrient Dense:

Having a lot of beneficial nutrients in relation to its calorie content. i.e. more nutrients, less calories.

Nutrition:

the process of providing or obtaining the food necessary for health and growth.

Nutrition and dietetic technicians, registered (NDTR):

are educated and trained at the technical level of nutrition and dietetics practice for the delivery of safe, culturally competent, quality food and nutrition services. They are nationally credentialed and are an integral part of health care and food service management teams. They work under the supervision of a registered dietitian nutritionist when in direct patient/client nutrition care; and they may work independently in providing general nutrition education to healthy populations.

Nutritionist:

a person who studies nutrition. They may or may not be credentialed. The term nutritionist, when combined with "registered dietitian" or "licensed dietitian", would be credentialed nutritionists.

Phytochemicals:

bioactive nutrient plant chemicals in fruits, vegetables, grains, and other plant foods that may provide desirable health benefits.

Registered dietitian nutritionists (RD/RDN):

Credentialed experts who use their nutrition expertise to help individuals make unique, positive lifestyle changes. They work throughout the community in hospitals, schools, public health clinics, nursing homes, fitness centers, food management, food industry, universities, research and private practice. RDNs are advocates for advancing the nutritional status of Americans and people around the world.
